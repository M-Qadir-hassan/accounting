Why Profit Doesn't Always Equal Cash: The Founder's Trap

Cash Flow

Michael Torres

CFO & Co-Founder · Oct 20, 2024 · 5 min read

It is the scenario every new business owner dreads. You look at your P&L statement at the end of the month, and it shows a healthy net profit. You high-five your co-founder. But then, you go to pay the bills, and the bank account is empty.

How is this possible? If I made money, where is it?

This is the fundamental difference between Profit and Cash Flow. Profit is an accounting theory; Cash is a reality. Understanding the gap between the two is often the difference between a business that scales and one that stalls.

The Core Difference

In accrual accounting (which most businesses should use), revenue is recognized when it is *earned*, not when the cash hits the bank.

  • Profit: Revenue minus Expenses (includes invoices sent but not paid).
  • Cash Flow: Money in minus Money out.

If you send a $10,000 invoice today with "Net 60" terms, your P&L says you made $10,000 this month. But your bank account won't see that money for two months. In the meantime, you still have to pay rent and payroll.

"Revenue is vanity, profit is sanity, but cash is king."

Where Did The Cash Go?

If you are profitable but broke, your cash is likely hiding in one of these three places:

1. Accounts Receivable

This is the most common culprit. If your customers pay you slowly, you are essentially acting as a bank, lending them money at 0% interest.

2. Inventory

Inventory is cash sitting on a shelf. It doesn't show up as an expense on the P&L until you actually sell the item (Cost of Goods Sold). But the cash left your bank account the moment you bought the stock.

3. Debt Payments

The principal portion of a loan payment does not appear on your P&L (only the interest does). So if you are paying down a large business loan, you are draining cash without lowering your "profit."

How to Fix It

To align your profit with your cash, you need to tighten your cash conversion cycle.

Start by shortening payment terms for clients (move from Net 30 to Due on Receipt). Secondly, negotiate longer payment terms with your own vendors. If you can collect money in 10 days and pay bills in 30 days, your cash flow will naturally turn positive.

Finally, stop looking at your P&L in isolation. Always review your Statement of Cash Flows alongside it. That report tells the true story of your business's health.
